Busting Common Myths About Landlord Insurance in Melbourne

Back to News
Sep 12, 2023
Landlord Insurance

According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, approximately 9.8 million households existed in Australia as of 2021, with 31% being renters. This means that over 3 million households depend on rental properties, representing a significant portion of the housing market. Property owners who rent out their assets need to understand the complexities of landlord insurance to ensure adequate protection for their investments.

Many property investors operate under misconceptions about what their insurance covers, leading to gaps in protection and potential financial exposure. Understanding the facts about landlord insurance is essential for making informed decisions about your rental investment.

Myth #1 . My Property Is Covered When Tenants Go on Holiday

This common misconception can lead to significant coverage gaps. Landlords should check with tenants about extended absences and verify their insurance policy terms accordingly. Insurance coverage often has restrictions based on how long a property remains unoccupied, which directly affects your protection during tenant vacancies.

Many insurers impose specific limitations during prolonged vacancy periods. Some policies may require notification if a property will be vacant for more than 30 days, while others may impose restrictions or exclusions if the property remains unoccupied for extended periods. The definition of "unoccupied" can vary by insurer . some count this from when the tenant leaves, while others measure from when you list the property for rental.

Failing to notify your insurer of extended absences could result in claim denial if damage occurs during the vacancy period. This is why transparent communication with your insurance provider about your tenancy arrangements is critical. If you anticipate lengthy vacancy periods between tenants, discuss this with your broker to understand your coverage options.

Myth #2 . Landlord Insurance Covers Loss of Rent in Any Event

Loss of rent protection is one of the most misunderstood aspects of landlord insurance. Not all events trigger coverage, and understanding the specific circumstances that activate this protection is essential for your financial planning.

Loss of rent protection typically applies only to specific, defined circumstances:

  • Tenant default on rent payments and subsequent eviction proceedings
  • Formal tenant eviction through legal processes
  • Tenant's death or hardship order affecting rental capacity
  • Unsafe living conditions resulting from insured events (fire, flood, natural disasters)

Importantly, if a property becomes unhabitable due to an insured peril and the tenant cannot occupy it, loss of rent coverage typically applies while repairs are conducted. However, coverage does not extend to normal market conditions, economic downturns, or periods of difficulty finding tenants.

For units and apartments, this distinction becomes even more critical. The body corporate's insurance generally covers only the building structure and common areas, not individual units. Unit owners must secure their own landlord insurance to protect their personal investment and cover their unit-specific liabilities and loss of rent scenarios.

Myth #3 . Insurance Coverage Is Set for Life

Insurance policies are not permanent arrangements . they require active management and periodic renewal. Most landlord insurance policies run for twelve months and must be renewed annually to maintain continuous coverage.

Insurers must provide landlords with a fortnight's notice that their policy is expiring, giving you time to review and renew before coverage lapses. However, relying on insurer reminders is not sufficient. Proactive landlords should monitor their policy expiration dates and initiate renewal discussions well in advance to ensure continuous coverage.

During the renewal process, it's critical to update your insurer about any changes to your property or rental arrangement. Landlords should keep insurers informed about renovations, changes from long-term to short-term rental arrangements (such as Airbnb conversions), and any changes in property circumstances that might affect risk profile. Failing to disclose material changes could result in claim denial or policy cancellation.

Your insurance needs may also evolve. Regular policy reviews ensure your coverage remains adequate as property values increase, rental income grows, or property use changes. What provided sufficient coverage five years ago may be inadequate today.

Myth #4 . Self-Managed Property Always Saves Money

While self-managing a property eliminates professional management fees, this approach may not be financially optimal when all factors are considered. Outsourcing property management costs between 4% and 15% of weekly rent plus GST . a seemingly substantial expense that deserves careful evaluation.

However, professional property management provides substantial benefits that often justify the investment:

  • Regular inspections and maintenance coordination ensure property condition and early problem detection
  • Emergency repairs are coordinated professionally, minimizing damage and tenant dissatisfaction
  • Professional understanding of landlord and tenant legal rights protects against costly disputes
  • Prompt action on rent arrears prevents extended payment defaults and recovery complexity
  • Compliance with rental laws reduces legal exposure and regulatory risk

Professional property managers also bring market knowledge about competitive rental rates, helping optimize your rental income. They handle tenant screening to improve the quality of your rental relationships and reduce vacancy rates. Additionally, management fees are tax-deductible, reducing your actual net cost.

When comparing self-management to professional management, consider the hidden costs: your time investment, potential legal fees from disputes, losses from tenant defaults, and opportunity cost of capital tied up in problem resolution. Professional management often pays for itself through improved tenant quality, higher rental rates, and faster vacancy fills.
