Is Your Construction Business Ready for La Niña?

Back to News
Oct 12, 2023
Construction Insurance

The construction sector is one of the most weather-dependent industries in Australia. With La Niña returning and bringing cooler, wetter conditions, construction companies face increased risks to their operations, timelines, and bottom line. Understanding these risks and securing appropriate insurance protection is critical for maintaining business stability during unpredictable weather patterns.

La Niña by the Numbers

According to the Bureau of Meteorology, La Niña has returned with above-average rainfall expected across Australia's east and north regions during spring and summer. The implications are significant and measurable. East coast storms and floods have led to almost $6 billion in insurance payouts in the past two years, highlighting the severity of weather impacts on the built environment.

These figures represent more than statistics . they reflect real disruptions to construction schedules, project delays, and financial losses across the industry. Construction companies that fail to adequately prepare and insure against these weather events face substantial business interruption and cash flow challenges that can threaten viability.

What Does La Niña Mean for Construction?

Construction companies represented over a quarter of Australian business insolvencies in 2021, driven by increased building costs, extended interest rates, supply chain disruptions, worker shortages, and extreme weather. The latest La Niña pattern threatens the recovering sector with multiple compounding challenges:

  • Delayed projects and schedule overruns causing contractual penalties
  • Cost blowouts from weather disruptions and emergency remediation
  • Reduced cash flow for trades and suppliers in the supply chain
  • Potential losses equaling 5% of annual turnover from weather-related events

These risks are not hypothetical. Builders and contractors must proactively address weather vulnerabilities in their business planning, risk management procedures, and insurance coverage to navigate La Niña conditions successfully.

Mitigating Wet-Weather Hazards

Construction sites face multiple specific risks during wet conditions that extend beyond simple delays. Understanding and addressing these hazards is essential for worker safety, site integrity, and legal compliance.

  • Excavation or trench collapse from waterlogged soil instability
  • Sediment runoff contaminating storm drains and waterways
  • Slip, trip, and fall hazards from mud and water accumulation
  • Loss of control of portable equipment in saturated ground conditions
  • Electrical incidents from water-damaged machinery and temporary power systems

Worksafe Victoria recommends comprehensive daily worksite risk assessments, ground stability checks, marking soft areas with flags or bollards, and maintaining worker communication systems. Site managers should implement specific protocols for high-risk weather periods, including designated safe zones, enhanced drainage systems, and contingency shelter arrangements.

Set Realistic Client Expectations

Builders should implement flexible scheduling practices to mitigate the commercial impact of weather delays. These practices include establishing realistic contingencies in project timelines, avoiding over-committing resources during uncertain weather periods, maintaining transparent communication with suppliers and clients about potential delays, addressing disputes promptly through clear contractual frameworks, and limiting fixed-price contracts during uncertain weather seasons.

Building strong relationships with clients through proactive communication about weather risks demonstrates professionalism and reduces disputes. Clear contractual provisions that address weather events help protect both builders and clients from unexpected financial exposure.

Construction Work Insurance

Also called Contract Works Insurance, this coverage protects building projects from multiple weather-related and other specified perils. Comprehensive construction work insurance covers storms, hail, cyclones, floods, accidental damage, fire, theft, and malicious damage to project materials and structures. It typically covers legal liability, material damage, and project losses from insured events, providing essential financial protection against the very risks La Niña conditions present.

For construction companies operating in Australia's variable climate, construction work insurance is not a luxury . it's a business essential. The coverage protects your investments, maintains cash flow during disruptions, and ensures your business can recover quickly from weather events.
